        Another quiet shift is happening in AI, and it’s bigger than it looks. Some of the most active AI research right now is no longer about making models smarter. It’s about making them act. We’re moving from AI that answers questions to AI that takes action across tools, systems and workflows. Instead of opening five apps, humans describe an outcome. Agents decide the steps. They interact with software. They execute. This is why “AI agents” are becoming the dominant concept across startups, big tech and research labs. They become resources AI can use on our behalf. The long-term implication is not faster tools. It’s fewer tools. Fewer dashboards. Fewer clicks. Less human coordination. The real question is not whether this will happen. It’s how quickly organizations will adapt to a world where work is delegated to machines, not performed inside software.         Agentic transformation of Enterprise IT platforms is happening faster than most realise!! One of the main reasons is the overcoming agent memory issues; strategies that are making AI agents true learners This paper here is extremely good! "Memory Management and Contextual Consistency for Long‑Running Low‑Code Agents": https://lnkd.in/djc-2kZX It tackles a key problem; when agents run over long periods, they suffer from “memory inflation” (too much data) and “contextual degradation” (losing relevance). The authors propose a hybrid memory system that combines episodic + semantic memory and uses an Intelligent Decay mechanism to prune and consolidate memory based on recency, relevance, and usefulness. This is huge for enterprise agents that need to run in complex environments for weeks, months, or years, like planning agents, supply chain agents, or manufacturing coordinators. This is super important for Agentic Resource Planning (ARP) systems: - Ensures long-term consistency in decision-making (agents don’t forget what they’ve learned) - Prevents bloat and irrelevant memory that degrades performance - Enables agents to remain “sharp,” lean, and reliable in real-world “always-on” operations The better memory architectures are, the less we’ll fight brittle systems and the more we can trust agents to think, plan, and act across time.
